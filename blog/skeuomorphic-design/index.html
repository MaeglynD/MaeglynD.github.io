What happened to Skeuomorphic Design?

For those who don't know skeuomorphic design was essentially what everyone used in order to communicate UI from the 80’s to early 2000’s. Objects in software would mimic their real life counterparts, take Apple's flower design for example. The old skeuomorphic design includes a real life sunflower whereas the new flat design does not.

But what happened to it? And why did flat design reign overwhelming victorious during changes in UI? Well for starters, in a 1980’s era where no one was familiar with operating a machine with a mouse nor had any clue how to operate a graphic user interface, the only point of reference would be real life images. Thus when designing UI’s, people would use realistic images. Many of the icons we know today would bear no meaning to people in 80's. Take the Window's / Apple's close, minimize and maximize buttons as an example. Most are adept to computers nowadays, so regardless of how much these markings change we’ll still understand they’re meaning.

Apple's venture into flat design started with IOS 7, which was launched in late 2013. This new flat design aesthetic was far more appealing than the skeuomorphic one, so why did it take so long to be introduced? A couple reasons, Apple’s retina display came out in June 2012, which drastically improved the definition of the UI. 326 pixels per inch, 4 times greater than its predecessor, now gave more clarity than ever before. In an interview with Craig Federighi on USA today, Craig noted that;

"Before, the shadowing effect we used was a great way to distract from the limitations of the display. But with a display thats this precise, there's nowhere to hide."

Which Implies that skeuomorphic design looks worse as displays improve. Window’s shift into flat design is also well known, with their 2012 release of Window's 8. Microsoft’s corporate vice president Michael Angiulo explained in a computex taipei talk that the driving force behind Window's 8’s creation was multi-platform compatibility. As we know now, microsoft heavily pushed tablets and detachable laptops into the market, all with different displays and viewports. It's worth noting that Window's 8 received mass backlash, however this was less flat designs fault, more literal functionality, from the removal of the start menu to pushing OS built in apps like skype which run slower.

Flat design’s potential for animations and vector graphics far exceeds the limitations of skeuomorphic design. Imagine creating the animation above using skeuomorphism. Scalable vector graphics (especially icons) are now the norm, and rose from the death of skeuomorphism. In conclusion, massive progressions in display technology gave convience for companies to completely rethink their UI.

Who pioneered Flat Design?

You can see examples of flat design being used all the way back in 2006 in Window’s zoom 30 UI. Which is furthermore evidence that the world wasn't ready for the switch just yet. The defining date in all companies seems to lie somewhere between 2011 to 2013, with Androids Honeycomb 3.0 released February 2011, Window's 8 released June 2012 and Apple IOS 7 released 2013. Despite being latest to the party, Apple clearly comes first in UI design and continues to do so even today. To learn more about Apple’s design and marketing i highly recommend their developers page.

Is it just a trend?

The term trend in design can't really be applied like it can be in fashion, due to functionality. You can rip as many holes as you like in your jeans but they’ll still be usable. Try doing this with your OS / software / app and you’ll make the user more frustrated. Having said that, many key design elements that were blacklisted to not be used during the flat design cross over are slowly being implemented back. Drop shadows in moderation are extremely useful to display elevation (Check out Material’s elevation page for a deeper insight). Gloss is being used in heavy moderation for click animations and gradients are recommended by Apple themselves when creating your app icon. From this we can infer that flat design isn't a trend, rather the next progression of a much needed change in design and is still being refined today. Until the next major feat in technology, its likely flat design is here to stay.
